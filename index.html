The More The Merrier

Information Transparency as a tool to Break Down Sovereignty

With reference to Black Panther and The Diplomat

These two seemingly innocent birds represent the two "actors" - internal and external influences. As they overlook the individual, capable of making decisions and exercising authority over his life, they gossip and judge him for his actions.

01

Glass walls of Information

As a child, I used to play pretend with my soft toys at home with my brother. As the self-appointed king, I quickly learned that sharing too much about my "kingdom" invited unwanted advice from my brother. I was fearful of offending him, so I had to figure out how to tell him off nicely while protecting my sovereignty. Looking back, that living room floor was a perfect microcosm of modern international relations. In our hyper-connected world, transparency is the currency of cooperation, but it comes at a steep price: vulnerability. The more a nation shares, the more it invites external interference. After watching Black Panther and The Diplomat, I find that true sovereignty is increasingly difficult to maintain when the walls of the "kingdom" become glass, leaving us—much like my younger self—struggling to govern without someone else trying to move the stuffed animals.

02

Dual Judgement

Greater information transparency invites external actors to judge the state’s performance and influence the state of affairs of a sovereign state, reducing the sovereignty of a country. Information transparency does not necessarily mean that the “truth is out of the bag” , it just means that the state is judged by two actors: internally by its people and externally by the rest of the world. For example, in Black Panther, Tchalla is judged internally by his people as their king and externally by the UN and the rest of the world as the leader of a poor third world country. Similarly, in The Diplomat, with greater interconnectedness within states, Kate was sent as an external and foreign diplomat to handle the crisis between UK and Iran, evidently interfering in the affairs of a sovereign state. These narratives highlight a modern paradox: transparency is both a bridge and a breach. By getting the world to look behind the curtain, a country has to trade autonomy to be included in the global world.

03

Diplomacy is Key

Ultimately as the lines between internal policy and external scrutiny continue to blur, sovereignty is no longer defined by total control, but by how skilfully a leader navigates the dual judgment of their own citizens and the global gallery. Whether it’s a fictional king in Wakanda or a diplomat in a real-world crisis, the lesson remains the same: in an interconnected world, sovereignty is becoming less and less important. And as ironic as it sounds, diplomacy – a convincing, elaborate performance by governments - is what keeps leaders navigating this interconnected, complicated game of cards today.
