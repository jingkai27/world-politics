The More The Merrier

Information Transparency as a tool to Break Down Sovereignty

With reference to Black Panther and The Diplomat

These two seemingly innocent birds represent the two "actors" - internal and external influences. As they overlook the individual, capable of making decisions and exercising authority over his life, they gossip and judge him for his actions.

01

Glass walls of Information

As a child, I used to play pretend with my soft toys at home with my brother. As the self-appointed king, I quickly learned that sharing too much about my "kingdom" invited unwanted advice from my brother. I was fearful of offending him, so I had to figure out how to tell him off nicely while protecting my sovereignty. Looking back, that living room floor was a perfect microcosm of modern international relations. In our hyper-connected world, transparency is the currency of cooperation, but it comes at a steep price: vulnerability. The more a nation shares, the more it invites external interference. After watching Black Panther and The Diplomat, I find that true sovereignty is increasingly difficult to maintain when the walls of the "kingdom" become glass, leaving us‚Äîmuch like my younger self‚Äîstruggling to govern without someone else trying to move the stuffed animals.

02

Dual Judgement

Greater information transparency invites external actors to judge the state‚Äôs performance and influence the state of affairs of a sovereign state, reducing the sovereignty of a country. Information transparency does not necessarily mean that the ‚Äútruth is out of the bag‚Äù , it just means that the state is judged by two actors: internally by its people and externally by the rest of the world. For example, in Black Panther, Tchalla is judged internally by his people as their king and externally by the UN and the rest of the world as the leader of a poor third world country. Similarly, in The Diplomat, with greater interconnectedness within states, Kate was sent as an external and foreign diplomat to handle the crisis between UK and Iran, evidently interfering in the affairs of a sovereign state. These narratives highlight a modern paradox: transparency is both a bridge and a breach. By getting the world to look behind the curtain, a country has to trade autonomy to be included in the global world.

03

Diplomacy is Key

Ultimately as the lines between internal policy and external scrutiny continue to blur, sovereignty is no longer defined by total control, but by how skilfully a leader navigates the dual judgment of their own citizens and the global gallery. Whether it‚Äôs a fictional king in Wakanda or a diplomat in a real-world crisis, the lesson remains the same: in an interconnected world, sovereignty is becoming less and less important. And as ironic as it sounds, diplomacy ‚Äì a convincing, elaborate performance by governments - is what keeps leaders navigating this interconnected, complicated game of cards today.

We are all humans - Media, Security and Borders

With reference to The Interview and Joint Security Area

After watching both shows, I couldn't help but think about this comic. What the media portrays may not always necessarily be true, but for the sake of borders and security, certain media stereotypes must be enforced to the public.

01

Different Countries, Same Narrative

I couldn‚Äôt help but notice how The Interview (2014) and Joint Security Area (2000), both referenced North Korea. Despite being set 14 years apart and filmed in different countries, both showed the same stereotype of North Korea ‚Äì a notorious, highly-monitored dictatorship with fearful and angry Koreans who will take revenge against US and Western ideology at any time. Yet both shows showed a different reality from the stereotype ‚Äì North Koreans are happy there, have the capacity to put differences aside to have a good time with Americans and South Koreans. This gave me a different point of view ‚Äì we are all humans, yet the discrepancies highlight how the media creates and exploits certain stereotypes of people to advance security interests, which may not always be true.

02

Innate Capabilities to Connect

For this section, I wanted a TV with this cartoon to represent this section. The room represents the sovereign state we live in, and the TV represents the media stereotypes that are presented to us. In both films, the media accurately represents the traditional beliefs of North Korea, but what touched me the most was how all North Koreans, from the supreme leader to the commoner, had the innate capacity to connect with their perceived ‚Äúenemies‚Äù ‚Äì both the United States and South Korea to forge and cultivate strong relations. Yet, they always had to suppress it, as the system, built on the fears of security, will eliminate them if they showed sympathy for their enemies.

03

Insights

This left me with the following thoughts:

  • Security as a tool for cohesion. Security is often a social construct utilized by those in power to maintain state relevance. If humans are innately tribal, the "need" for security becomes the glue that binds a community. In the absence of an active threat, "enemies" are often manufactured to justify the state's role as a protector.
  • Borders serve as the physical manifestation of the security narrative. They don‚Äôt just mark territory. With a clear divide between ‚Äúme‚Äù and ‚Äúthem‚Äù, making the media‚Äôs caricatures of the ‚Äúother‚Äù more believable.
  • Media as a Force Multiplier: When media amplify security concerns, they amplify primal fears, creating a feedback loop where the populace accepts suppression and state control in exchange for the illusion of safety from a stereotyped enemy.

04

Where do we draw the line?

Ultimately, The Interview and JSA reveal a similar paradox: while individuals possess an innate desire for cross-border connection, the structures of sovereignty often depend on the suppression of that very empathy. In my 3D room, the TV serves as a constant flicker of 'security-driven' narratives that keep us isolated within our own walls. If we recognize that these 'enemies' are often constructs designed to maintain state cohesion, we begin to see the borders not as shields, but as barriers to a more authentic global community. Security should protect people, not just the systems that govern them.
Are the boundaries we respect protecting us, or are they simply keeping us from a connection that the system fears?
